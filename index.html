Alan Turing

The father of modern computing

Alan Turing at age 16

Here's a timeline of Turing's life:

  • 1912 - Born in Maida Vale, London
  • 1918 - Joins St Michael’s day school in Hastings, where he does not do very well. He is sent to Hazelhurst Preparatory School where he learns to play chess.
  • 1926 - Becomes a student at Sherbone School in D orset. He is not interested in their traditional classical education as he really wants to spend his time st udying Science and Mathematics.
  • 1927 - Becomes deeply interested in the work of Albe rt Einstein and is able to develop work done by Einstein based on a questioning of Newton’s Laws of Motion
  • 1934 - Graduates with distinction in Mathematics from King’s College, Cambridge
  • 1938 - Goes to Princeton University in America to study mathematics and is awarded a PhD
  • 1939 - Is asked to join the Government Codes and Ciphers School and arrives at Bletchley Park the day after war is declared
  • 1940 - With Gordon Welchman, he develops the Bombe to decipher the messages sent by the Germans using their Enigma machine.
  • 1943 - Works with top people in the USA, which he visits to share information on code breaking
  • 1945 - At the end of the war, he is awarded the OBE for his wartime services
  • 1946 - Publishes a paper with the first detailed design of a stored-program computer
  • 1949 - Is made deputy director of the Computing Laboratory at Manchester University
  • 1950 - Publishes the famous paper ‘Computer Machinery and Intelligence’ in which he develops the Turing Test
  • 1951 - Is elected Fellow of the Royal Society, and also gives talks about Artificial Intelligence on BBC
  • 1952 - Arrested for gross indecency. He loses his security clearance so he cannot work. He is offered chemical treatment instead of going to prison. The treatment makes him very unwell.
  • 1954 - Found dead in his home in Wilmslow, Cheshire, from poisoning. A half-eaten apple was found next to him laced with cyanide.

“This man was a national treasure and we hounded him to his death... I'm looking for an apology from the British government because that's where I think the wrong was done. But Turing is clearly someone of international stature.”

John Graham-Cumming, computer scientist
